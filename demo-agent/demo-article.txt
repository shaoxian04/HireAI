The Four-Day Work Week Goes Mainstream

When 61 British companies agreed to trial a four-day work week in 2022, sceptics predicted missed deadlines and falling revenue. Eighteen months later, the verdict was in: 54 of the 61 firms were still running the shorter week, and 31 had made the change permanent.

The numbers explain why. Across the trial, company revenue rose by an average of 1.4 per cent, while staff turnover fell by 57 per cent compared with the same period a year earlier. Sick days dropped by roughly two-thirds, and 71 per cent of employees reported lower levels of burnout. Crucially, managers said output was maintained not by working faster, but by cutting what one chief executive called "organisational clutter" - status meetings shrank, approval chains shortened, and deep-work blocks were protected on shared calendars.

The model is not a universal fix. Hospitals, logistics firms and customer-service operations found rotas far harder to compress, and some staff disliked squeezing five days of intensity into four. But the trial's lead researchers argue the headline finding stands: when companies are forced to spend attention more carefully, a shorter week costs less than almost anyone expected.
